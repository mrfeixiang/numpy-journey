Why Professor Nam Won't Let Me Use Pandas

The hidden philosophy behind an "old-school" teaching method

PROLOGUE

A Researcher Who Can't Code Walks Into a Lab

Let me be honest with you from the start: I am not a computer science student. I am not a developer. I am not even someone who had ever opened a terminal window before this story begins.

I am a drug response prediction researcher. My world is biology, molecules, and clinical outcomes. Code was something other people wrote — something that happened behind the curtain.

Then Professor Nam handed me three CSV files and said:

"Merge these. With Python. You have one week." — Professor Nam, without blinking

Three files. Simple enough, right?

The Files:
Padel.csv — 217 rows × 2,326 columns (molecular descriptors)
Mutation.csv — 21,213 rows × 1,002 columns (genetic mutations)
Response.csv — 194,750 rows × 3 columns (drug response data)

I stared at the numbers. Two thousand three hundred and twenty-six columns. Twenty-one thousand rows. My Excel instincts told me this was going to crash the moment I double-clicked the file.

"Can I use ChatGPT?" I asked.

"You can. But you won't understand what it gives you. And in this lab, understanding is not optional." — Professor Nam

So I made a decision. No AI. Only Google. Only my own hands on the keyboard. If I was going to learn this, I would learn it honestly.

Some people want to understand everything. I am not that ambitious. I only need 0.1 — but it must be real.

DAY 1 – 2

First Line of Code, Hands Shaking

I remember my hands. They were shaking. Not from cold — from the terrifying blankness of a Jupyter notebook. That blinking cursor felt like it was judging me.

I typed my very first line of Python: 

import numpy as np

Nothing happened. No fanfare. No congratulations. Just... silence. I suppose that's how programming works. You whisper into the void and hope nothing screams back.

Then I tried to create my first array:

TypeError
np.zeros(3, 4)
TypeError: Cannot interpret '4' as a data type

I stared at this for twenty minutes. Why? Why can't you just make me a 3×4 grid of zeros? What is so hard about this?

The fix was absurdly simple:

np.zeros((3, 4))   # A tuple! It needs a TUPLE!

One pair of parentheses. That was the difference between failure and success. I spent fifteen minutes understanding why (5,) is a tuple but (5) is just the number 5 in parentheses. Fifteen minutes. On a comma.

Lesson learned: In NumPy, shape is always a tuple. (3, 4) means 3 rows, 4 columns. The parentheses are not decoration — they are the grammar of the language.

The Axis Crisis

Then came axis.

axis=0 means rows, right? So if I do np.sum(arr, axis=0), it sums the rows? Wrong. It sums along axis 0, which means it collapses the rows and gives you column totals.

I literally stood up from my desk. I walked to the window. I stared at the campus parking lot for five minutes. How can something so simple be so confusing?

Then I found it — a Real Python article that changed everything. One word: "collapses."

The Eureka Reframe:
axis=0 doesn't mean "do something to the rows."
It means "collapse axis 0" — the rows disappear, columns survive.
axis=1 means "collapse axis 1" — the columns disappear, rows survive. 
# The axis diagram that saved my sanity
#
#     axis=1 (columns) ───►
#     ┌───┬───┬───┬───┐
# a   │ 1 │ 2 │ 3 │ 4 │  axis=0
# x   ├───┼───┼───┼───┤    │
# i   │ 5 │ 6 │ 7 │ 8 │    │
# s   ├───┼───┼───┼───┤    ▼
# =   │ 9 │10 │11 │12 │  (rows)
# 0   └───┴───┴───┴───┘
#
# np.sum(arr, axis=0) → collapses rows    → [15, 18, 21, 24]
# np.sum(arr, axis=1) → collapses columns → [10, 26, 42]

I wrote this diagram by hand in my notebook. I drew it three times. On the third drawing, it clicked. Axis doesn't point to what you keep. It points to what you destroy.

"The moment you stop thinking 'axis=0 means rows' and start thinking 'axis=0 collapses rows,' NumPy stops being confusing." — Real Python (paraphrased)

DAY 3
🤯 🧊 💔

The Day NaN Broke My Worldview

Day 3 was the day I learned that mathematics, as I understood it, was a lie.

I was cleaning data. Some cells were empty. NumPy represented them as NaN — "Not a Number." Fine. Makes sense. Missing data is not a number. I can accept that.

Then I typed this: 

np.nan == np.nan
# False

False.

NaN is not equal to itself. A thing — is not equal to itself.

I checked again. I checked three times. I Googled it. And I found the IEEE 754 standard — the international specification for floating-point arithmetic, written in 1985, which explicitly declares:

IEEE 754 Rule: NaN shall compare unequal to everything, including itself. This is by design, not a bug.

By design. Someone — a committee of very serious mathematicians and computer scientists — sat in a room and decided that some things should not be equal to themselves. 

# The right way to check for NaN:
np.isnan(np.nan)    # True

# The wrong way (always returns False):
x == np.nan        # False, even if x IS NaN

I sat with this for a long time. A thing not equal to itself — there is something strangely romantic about that. Like a philosophical statement disguised as a floating-point rule. Some things in this world are so undefined, so absent, that they cannot even claim identity with themselves.

I wrote in my lab notebook that night:

"NaN is not a number. It is an absence wearing a mask. And the mask doesn't even match itself." — my lab notebook, Day 3
Practical takeaway: Always use np.isnan() to check for NaN. Never use ==. This one rule will save you hours of silent, invisible bugs.

The Real Boss: Merging Data with Purpose

Days 1 through 3 were tutorials. Day 4 was when the real work began.

I had three CSV files. I needed to merge them into one unified matrix. Professor Nam had said to use NumPy, but to understand the merge logic, I first needed to know what I was merging.

The Silent Rename Failure

I tried pd.merge() — yes, I broke Professor Nam's rule and peeked at Pandas. Just a peek. And it punished me immediately. 

merged = pd.merge(df1, df2, on='drug_id')
# No error. No warning. Just... wrong data.
# Columns silently renamed: response_x, response_y
# Rows silently dropped. No notification.
Silent Failure — The Worst Kind

Pandas didn't crash. It didn't throw an error. It just quietly renamed my columns and silently dropped rows that didn't match. I didn't notice for two hours.

Two hours of working with wrong data. Two hours of trusting a tool that was lying to me with a smile.

That was the moment I understood why Professor Nam wanted me to use NumPy. NumPy doesn't do magic. If something is wrong, it screams. Pandas whispers, and sometimes it whispers lies.

The Transpose: 4-Step Process

Back to NumPy. The problem: my data was in the wrong orientation. Drugs were in rows where they should have been in columns. Cell lines were columns where they should have been rows. I needed to transpose — and not just flip, but restructure. 

# Step 1: Extract the data (no headers)
data = arr[1:, 1:]

# Step 2: Transpose the matrix
data_T = data.T
print(data_T.shape)  # Rows and columns swapped

# Step 3: Re-attach headers (swapped)
new_col_headers = arr[1:, 0]   # Old row labels → new column headers
new_row_headers = arr[0, 1:]   # Old column headers → new row labels

# Step 4: Reconstruct with np.concatenate
result = np.concatenate([new_row_headers.reshape(-1,1), data_T], axis=1)

Four steps. Each one demanded that I understand what was happening to the shape of my data. There was no .pivot_table() to hide behind. Every reshape, every concatenation, I had to justify with logic.

Day 5: The Moon Landing

On Day 5, I typed every step by hand. No copy-pasting. No templates. Just me, NumPy, and three CSV files that had haunted me for four days.

I loaded the files. I extracted the keys. I matched them manually with np.isin() and boolean indexing. I transposed. I concatenated. I checked every shape at every step.

And then I ran the final cell: 

print(final_matrix.shape)
# (there it was)
# 23538 columns

23,538 columns.

All three files. Merged. Aligned. Correct.

I won't pretend I didn't feel something. My eyes got wet. Not from sadness — from something I didn't expect to feel while staring at a number on a screen. It was the feeling of arriving somewhere after walking every step of the road.

"That was my moon landing moment. Not because 23,538 columns is impressive. But because I understood every single one of them." — my lab notebook, Day 5, 11:47 PM

Neil Armstrong had 400,000 engineers. I had Google and a stubborn refusal to ask a chatbot.

PHILOSOPHY

Why NumPy, Not Pandas — Professor Nam's Method

After seven days, I finally understood. Professor Nam wasn't being old-fashioned. He wasn't ignorant of Pandas. He wasn't punishing me. He was teaching me how to think.

  • Pandas is a black box. NumPy is transparent.
    When you use pd.merge(), you trust the library to make decisions for you — which columns to match, which rows to drop, how to handle duplicates. With NumPy, every decision is yours. Every row that appears in the final matrix is there because you put it there.
  • Shape thinking is the foundation of data science.
    Before you can use any tool — TensorFlow, PyTorch, scikit-learn — you need to think in shapes. (217, 2326) is not just two numbers. It is a mental image: 217 drugs described by 2,326 features. NumPy forces you to see this. Pandas hides it behind column names.
  • Transpose is the watershed of matrix thinking.
    If you can transpose a matrix and reconstruct its headers — if you can see in your mind what .T does to every element — you understand matrices. This is the dividing line between someone who uses tools and someone who understands data.
  • Debug ability is more important than writing code.
    NumPy errors are loud, ugly, and honest. ValueError: shapes not aligned tells you exactly what went wrong. Pandas errors are often silent — your code runs, but your data is quietly corrupted. Professor Nam wanted me to learn from honest errors, not to be betrayed by silent ones.
  • Desensitization training — tools change, matrix logic doesn't.
    Pandas might be replaced next year. A new library might emerge. But matrices? Linear algebra? Shape arithmetic? Those are permanent. By learning NumPy, I wasn't learning a tool — I was learning the language that all tools speak.

NumPy Approach

You see every step. You control every merge. Errors are loud. Understanding is mandatory.

Pandas Shortcut

Convenience first. Decisions hidden. Errors often silent. Understanding optional.

"It's like learning to drive manual before automatic. You don't need to drive manual for the rest of your life. But once you've felt the clutch, you understand what the car is actually doing." — Professor Nam's analogy

On our last meeting that week, I asked Professor Nam to summarize his teaching philosophy. He thought for a moment. Then he said:

"I don't teach NumPy. I don't teach Pandas. I teach how to figure things out when you don't understand." — Professor Nam

I wrote it down. I underlined it twice. I think about it every time I open a notebook.

EPILOGUE

A Letter to the Next Beginner

Dear reader who just opened your first Jupyter notebook,

I know the cursor is blinking. I know the emptiness feels enormous. I know you're thinking about asking an AI to write the code for you.

Don't.

Not because AI is bad. It's extraordinary. But right now, in this moment, you don't need extraordinary. You need struggle. You need that TypeError that makes no sense. You need that fifteen minutes spent on a single comma. You need that walk to the window where you stare at nothing and then suddenly, quietly, understand.

Don't use AI as a crutch during learning. A crutch helps you walk, but it also makes your legs forget how. Right now, your legs are learning. Let them stumble.

Here's what I wish someone had told me on Day 1:

Errors are your best teacher. Every red traceback is a lesson delivered at exactly the moment you need it. Don't fear them. Read them. They are more honest than any tutorial.

Google is your friend. Not your crutch — your friend. The difference is that a friend helps you understand; a crutch does the work for you. Search. Read. Try. Fail. Search again. This cycle is not inefficiency. It is learning.

The pride is yours alone. When you finally see those 23,538 columns appear — when you type print(shape) and the number is right — no one else will feel what you feel. No AI can share that moment with you. It belongs only to you, and to every error that led you there.

The code is short. The journey is long. But the understanding is yours forever.

— A fellow beginner, seven days ahead of you
